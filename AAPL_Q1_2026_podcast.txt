Apple Earnings Breakdown — Q1 Fiscal 2026
A Two-Host Deep Dive

HOSTS: ALEX and MORGAN

---

[INTRO]

ALEX: Welcome back. I'm Alex.

MORGAN: And I'm Morgan. Today we're breaking down Apple's fiscal first quarter of 2026 — the quarter ending December 2025. And Apple, just… had a moment.

ALEX: They really did. Tim Cook opened the call with this: "I am proud to say that we just had a quarter for the record books."

MORGAN: That's not standard CEO boilerplate. "Quarter for the record books." And then he backed it up: $143.8 billion in revenue. Up 16% from a year ago.

ALEX: EPS of $2.84 — up 19%. All-time record. Operating cash flow of $53.9 billion — all-time record. They set all-time revenue records in the Americas, Europe, Japan, and rest of Asia Pacific.

MORGAN: And the thing that everyone was watching — iPhone — Cook said "the demand for iPhone was simply staggering." Revenue of $85.3 billion, up 23% year-over-year.

ALEX: 23%. That's not a normal iPhone cycle. What's going on there?

MORGAN: Okay, so Cook explained why they exited the quarter with very lean channel inventory — and I want to quote him here because he was specific about it: "We exited the December quarter with very lean channel inventory due to that staggering level of demand. And based on that, we're in a supply chase mode to meet the very high levels of customer demand."

ALEX: A supply chase mode. So going into the March quarter, they're actually constrained on iPhone supply?

MORGAN: Correct. And he was candid about what's causing it: "The constraints that we have are driven by the availability of the advanced nodes that our SOCs are produced on."

ALEX: That's the chip manufacturing. So not an assembly problem — a foundry problem. Advanced node capacity.

MORGAN: Right. And he added: "We're seeing less flexibility in supply chain than normal, partly because of our increased demand." It's a good problem to have, but it's still a real constraint that shows up in the guidance.

ALEX: Which we'll get to. But first — I want to stay on iPhone for a second, because it wasn't just the U.S. The China number was eye-popping.

MORGAN: Greater China up 38% year over year. Cook said it was "driven by iPhone, where we set an all-time revenue record. So it was the best iPhone quarter in history in Greater China."

ALEX: What drove that specifically?

MORGAN: He pointed to a few things. Traffic in Apple stores in China grew by strong double digits year over year. The installed base hit an all-time high in both Greater China and mainland China. All-time record for upgraders. Strong double-digit growth on switchers. And then this stat: "According to a survey from WorldPanel, iPhones were the top three smartphones in urban China during the quarter."

ALEX: Top three. That's remarkable given how competitive that market is with Huawei and the local brands.

MORGAN: And Kevin Parekh — the CFO — added that iPad was the top tablet model in urban China, MacBook Air was the top-selling laptop model, and Mac Mini was the top-selling desktop model. So it wasn't just iPhone. Apple had a dominant December across their whole product lineup in China.

ALEX: Okay, let's talk services, because $30 billion is a number that needs its own moment.

MORGAN: All-time services revenue record. Up 14% year-over-year. Kevin was clear about how broad-based it was: "We had all-time records in advertising, cloud services, music, and payment services with December quarter records on the App Store and video."

ALEX: So when people ask "is the services business slowing down?" — not in this quarter.

MORGAN: And the flywheel behind it: 2.5 billion active devices in the installed base. That's a new all-time high. Kevin made the point explicitly — "With our install base of over 2.5 billion active devices, we have an incredibly strong foundation for new growth opportunities."

ALEX: That number has real strategic weight. The bigger the base, the more people you can sell subscriptions to, the more advertiser inventory you have, the more payment volume flows through Apple Pay.

MORGAN: On advertising specifically, he confirmed new ad slots coming to search in the App Store: "It provides advertisers more ways to be discovered." They didn't elaborate much, but that's incremental revenue on top of what's already a record business.

---

[SECTION: THE GOOGLE DEAL]

ALEX: Now here's the one that I think everyone was chewing on — Apple confirmed it's collaborating with Google to develop the next generation of Apple Foundation Models. Which is what will power a more personalized version of Siri.

MORGAN: Tim Cook's framing of this was interesting. An analyst from Melius, Ben Ritzes, asked directly how this decision was made, and Cook said: "We basically determined that Google's AI technology would provide the most capable foundation for Apple Foundation Models. And we believe that we can unlock a lot of experiences and innovate in a key way due to the collaboration."

ALEX: And on privacy — which is always the follow-up with Apple — he said they'll "continue to run on the device and run in private cloud compute and maintain our industry-leading privacy standards in doing so."

MORGAN: So the architecture stays the same — on-device processing, private cloud compute — but the underlying model capability is now being co-developed with Google's Gemini technology.

ALEX: Ritzes also asked whether there was a revenue-sharing component to this deal the way there is with Google Search.

MORGAN: And Cook's answer was basically: not telling you. "We're not releasing the details of that." Ritzes' response on the call was "Bummer. Okay. Well, I tried."

ALEX: Which Cook followed with: "You did." There's a lightness to how he handles those dead ends.

MORGAN: Another analyst — Krish Sankar from TD Cowen — pushed on this from a product angle. How should we think about Apple Foundation Models versus third-party models, is there a layer separation there? Cook kept it simple: "You should think of it as a collaboration. And we'll obviously independently continue to do some of our own stuff. But you should think of what is going to power the personalized version of Siri as a collaboration with Google."

ALEX: So Apple's own on-device intelligence stays, but the more advanced, personalized Siri is Google-powered. That's a big admission that they needed outside help on the frontier model side.

MORGAN: And the broader statement from Cook on AI — from his prepared remarks — is worth reading in full because it's actually a pretty strong claim: "We are bringing intelligence to more of what people already love about our products, so we can make every experience even more capable and effortless."

ALEX: Not "we're launching an AI product." It's "we're making everything you already use better."

MORGAN: And the installed base stat connects back here. If you have 2.5 billion active devices with Apple Intelligence enabled on the newer hardware, the distribution advantage is massive.

---

[SECTION: MEMORY PRICES AND GROSS MARGIN]

ALEX: Okay, the gnarly part. Memory prices are rising, everyone knows it, and multiple analysts asked about it. Let's lay out what Cook and Parekh actually said.

MORGAN: Cook set the table: "We do continue to see market pricing for memory increasing significantly. As always, we'll look at a range of options to deal with that."

ALEX: Range of options. He was asked multiple times to be more specific about what that means — LTAs, spot contracts, pricing lever — and he basically declined each time.

MORGAN: To the pricing question specifically: "I wouldn't want to speculate on that one." So no signal on whether Apple would pass memory cost increases through to consumers.

ALEX: Now, here's the thing — despite all this memory anxiety, the gross margin guidance for the March quarter is 48% to 49%. Ben Ritzes from Melius literally said on the call: "I'm pretty shocked. I've got to hand it to you, Tim."

MORGAN: And Kevin explained how: two things are going on. First, a strong iPhone cycle creates favorable mix — when iPhone sells well, you get mix and leverage benefits because iPhone contributes more than average to margins. Second, Services continues to grow at 14% and carries a 76.5% gross margin. So those two tailwinds are absorbing the memory headwind.

ALEX: Kevin was explicit about Q1: "We landed at 48.2%, so just above the high end of the range. Products gross margin was 40.7%, up 450 basis points sequentially, driven by favorable mix and leverage." For context, that's an unusually large sequential jump in product margins.

MORGAN: And on the going-forward question — Q2, 48 to 49 — Kevin said: "There are going to be a few puts and takes. We do expect to see favorable mix in the services. As you know, when we move from Q1 to Q2, that tends to be the case, and that's partly offset by a seasonal loss of leverage."

ALEX: So it's not that memory doesn't matter — Cook confirmed it will hit Q2 margins more than Q1 — but the headwind is being cushioned by the iPhone cycle and Services growth.

MORGAN: One more point Kevin made: they had a $1.4 billion tariff cost in Q1 and "landed roughly in that range." So tariffs are also baked into the numbers. The 48 to 49 guidance isn't pre-tariff optimism — it's with all of that in mind.

---

[SECTION: GUIDANCE]

ALEX: March quarter outlook: total company revenue up 13% to 16% year-over-year.

MORGAN: Kevin flagged that the guidance "comprehends our best estimates of constrained iPhone supply during the quarter." So they've already haircut the iPhone number for supply limitations. The question is whether the constraint gets resolved — or not — and how that tracks against demand.

ALEX: Services expected to grow at a similar rate to what they reported in December — which was 14%.

MORGAN: Gross margin: 48 to 49%. OpEx between $18.4 billion and $18.7 billion — roughly flat with Q1, driven by higher R&D year over year. Tax rate around 17.5%.

ALEX: And they declared a $0.26 dividend per share.

MORGAN: The other macro comment worth noting — Kevin added a specific caveat at the top of guidance: "This includes the types of forward-looking information…which assumes that global tariff rates, policies, and their application remain in effect as of this call, and the global macroeconomic outlook does not worsen from today."

ALEX: So the guidance is conditional on the tariff environment not getting worse. That's a meaningful hedge given where trade policy is right now.

---

[SECTION: CAPITAL RETURN]

MORGAN: Real quick on capital return — they returned nearly $32 billion to shareholders in a single quarter. That included $25 billion in open market share repurchases of 93 million shares, plus $3.9 billion in dividends.

ALEX: And they ended the quarter with net cash of $54 billion. They had $145 billion in total cash and marketable securities, with $91 billion in total debt. They're making progress on the path to cash-neutral, but they're still carrying a meaningful net cash cushion.

MORGAN: Number one: how fast does the supply constraint on iPhone clear. Cook said it's hard to predict when supply and demand will balance. MORGAN: Which is honest. iPhone 17 wasn't an AI story. It was a product story. The AI story is being layered on top of that installed base now, with the Google collaboration being the next chapter.
