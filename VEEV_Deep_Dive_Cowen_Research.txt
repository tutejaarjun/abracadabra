Fourth, the replatforming risk with moving CRM to Vault. Any time you're migrating thousands of customers off one platform onto another, there's execution risk.

And fifth, concerns about the biotech funding environment. A weak funding environment for small biotechs could slow adoption of clinical trial software tools. However, the report viewed this risk as overblown, noting that 82 percent of respondents in Cowen's biopharma R&D leader survey expected their R&D budgets to increase in 2023.

ALEX: What was Cowen's overall view?

MORGAN: They initiated coverage with a Market Perform rating and a 184 dollar price target, based on a 10-year discounted cash flow analysis using a 10 percent discount rate and 5 percent terminal growth rate. The stock was trading at about 167 dollars at the time.

Their view was essentially: great company, strong long-term fundamentals, but the current valuation was fairly reflecting those fundamentals. They saw Veeva trading at about 26 times calendar year 2023 estimated EBITDA, which was roughly in line with SaaS peers at about 28 times.

The thesis was that near-term growth was slowing as a function of CRM maturation and macro headwinds, and these were fairly priced. But looking further out, the reacceleration from R&D growth and data analytics could make the valuation more constructive heading into fiscal year 2025 and beyond.

ALEX: What did their long-term model look like?

MORGAN: They projected Veeva could reach adjusted operating margins of about 58 percent by fiscal year 2032, driven by scale benefits in the R&D segment, reduced investment intensity, and the gross margin uplift from eliminating Salesforce licensing fees once CRM moves to Vault. Free cash flow was projected to compound significantly, going from about 755 million in fiscal 2023 to over 1.5 billion by fiscal 2027 in their model.

The DCF terminal value implied an equity value of about 30 billion dollars, which worked out to 184 dollars per share.

ALEX: Let's bring it all together. If you had to explain Veeva's strategic position to someone who knows nothing about this company, what would you say?

MORGAN: I'd say Veeva is a company that made a bet 15 years ago that the life sciences industry needed its own purpose-built cloud software, and that bet has paid off spectacularly. They started with CRM — the tool pharma reps use every day — and used that as a wedge to build trust with the industry. Once they were inside the building, they expanded into data, analytics, clinical operations, regulatory, quality, and safety. They now have products that touch nearly every major workflow in a pharma company's operations.

The result is a business with 80 percent recurring revenue, 75 percent gross margins, nearly 40 percent operating margins, zero debt, and a customer base that is buying more products every year. They have 80 percent share in their core CRM market and are rapidly gaining share in R&D solutions.

ALEX: And the risks?

MORGAN: The risks are real but manageable. CRM seat reduction is a structural headwind, but it's being offset by data and analytics growth. The Salesforce-to-Vault migration is a big move, but management has planned a multi-year transition. Macro headwinds can slow certain products, but Veeva's core subscription contracts provide revenue visibility. And competition from Medidata, IQVIA, and Oracle is intense, but Veeva's integration advantage and customer trust give it a strong position.

ALEX: The thing that stands out to me is the culture piece. You have a founder-CEO who talks to customers every single day. You have a company that voluntarily became a Public Benefit Corporation. And you have a management team that consistently under-promises and over-delivers on targets. Those aren't just nice-to-have qualities. In an industry where trust and long-term relationships drive purchasing decisions, that culture is a genuine competitive moat.

MORGAN: Absolutely. Pharma companies are making decade-long bets when they choose a clinical trial management system or a CRM platform. They need to know that the vendor will be around, will keep investing, and will never cut corners on quality or compliance. Gassner's leadership style — deeply technical, deeply customer-focused, thinking in 10-to-20-year horizons — fits that need perfectly.

ALEX: And the financial model supports it. When you generate that much free cash flow with zero debt, you can invest in new products, make acquisitions, grow headcount, and weather downturns without ever needing to go to the debt markets or dilute shareholders excessively.

MORGAN: The last thing I'd add is the industry tailwind. The number of clinical trials is growing. Medicine is getting more complex. Pharma marketing is shifting from in-person rep visits to digital and omnichannel strategies. Regulatory requirements are getting more demanding. All of these trends create demand for exactly the kind of specialized cloud software that Veeva sells. The 13 billion dollar TAM today growing to 20 billion by 2030 isn't a stretch — it's a natural consequence of these secular trends.

ALEX: And if Veeva can go from 15 percent penetration in R&D to even 25 or 30 percent, while maintaining its commercial dominance and scaling the data and analytics products, the math gets very compelling over a multi-year horizon.

MORGAN: That's exactly the bull case. Near-term, the growth is modest — mid-teens — as CRM matures and R&D ramps. But the long-term trajectory points to a much larger, more profitable business. The question for investors is just: are you getting a good enough price for that long-term trajectory? At the time of this report, Cowen said the price was fair. Not cheap, not expensive. Fair.

MORGAN: Agreed. There aren't many companies that dominate an industry this completely while growing this profitably. Veeva is one of those rare businesses where the moat is simultaneously the product, the customer relationships, and the regulatory complexity of the industry itself. You don't replicate that easily.
